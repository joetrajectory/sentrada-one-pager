DATE: June 2026

EDITION LINE: Venture Capital and SaaS Leadership / London, New York, San Francisco, Berlin

HEADLINE: $1bn Raised, 53 SaaS Companies, One Recurring Question: Who Runs Sales?

STAT: 447 Open roles across the MMC Ventures portfolio | Source: jobs.mmc.vc, June 2026

LEAD ARTICLE: When MMC Ventures promoted three members of its investment team in early 2022, the firm simultaneously confirmed it had raised over $1bn in cumulative funding across its vehicles. Twenty-five years after Bruce Macfarlane, Alan Morgan and Allan Cockell founded the firm as an Edinburgh angel syndicate, MMC had become one of Britain's most active early-stage technology investors, with 53 SaaS companies in a portfolio of 144 and a research arm whose State of AI report helped define the European AI investment landscape. Its most recent research, the State of Agentic AI: Founder's Edition, published in November 2025, surveyed more than 30 agentic AI startup founders across Europe. The platform, by most measures, works.

But across that portfolio, a pattern keeps surfacing that neither research reports nor operational playbooks have resolved. When a B2B SaaS company closes its Series A and needs to hire its first senior sales leader, the probability of getting it right first time remains stubbornly low. And when the hire does not work, the cost is not a recruitment fee. It is six to twelve months of the runway that MMC helped provide.

The evidence is in public announcements. APEXX Global, the payment orchestration platform in MMC's portfolio, confirmed in April 2026 that Simon Hughes had been promoted to Chief Commercial Officer and Simon Eve appointed Head of Sales and Partnerships, joining from Trustly. The restructure followed an earlier commercial configuration. In August 2025, Tyk, the API management platform backed by MMC, appointed Mark Gurvis as VP Sales Americas alongside two other senior hires, with CEO Martin Buhr describing them as 'API industry veterans' brought in to serve the US financial sector. SenseOn, whose Series A was led by MMC, has been through a similar transition. CEO David Atkinson said in a published interview that his company had 'learned the hard way how to hire the right sales talent at the right time.'

These are not failures. They are corrections. Technically gifted founders building complex enterprise products are asked to evaluate a discipline they have never practised, and the result follows a familiar pattern: a candidate who carries the right title and revenue line on their CV but who is building for a sales motion the company has not yet earned.

MMC has acknowledged the gap more directly than most funds. The firm's CONNECT platform delivers portfolio support across seven operational pillars, and Talent is one of them. The portfolio job board at jobs.mmc.vc currently carries 447 open roles. A separate Talent Sharing Community pools candidates across the network. David Benigson, CEO of Signal AI, stated on the MMC website that the firm 'helped shape our commercial strategy and team, from KPIs and hiring to recommending a sales leader.' Sam Fromson, co-founder and COO of YuLife, said MMC had introduced 'more sales prospects than any of our other investors.'

Oliver Richards, General Partner at MMC and a board member at Peak AI, YuLife and Copper, has written that portfolio companies 'don't have to hyper-scale the team to deliver rapid commercial growth' and that 'remarkable growth' can come from 'maintaining a relatively small team.' The argument is not for more salespeople. It is for the right one, placed at the right moment, with the sector expertise and stage-appropriate playbook that the founder cannot assess alone.

For a firm that has built a $1bn platform on the conviction that it can identify exceptional founders, the question of who leads sales for those founders is no longer an operational detail. It is a portfolio-level variable. The funds that solve it systematically, ensuring the right commercial leader is in place before the post-funding clock starts, will compound across every investment. The rest will keep learning the hard way.
